Sayan Patra
My work focuses on neuromorphic computing as a computational substrate for next‑generation intelligent systems. I investigate spiking neural dynamics and event‑driven computation for processing high-dimensional sensory data, particularly in vision, tactile sensing, and spatial perception. My research examines the integration of biologically inspired architectures with geometric machine learning on non‑Euclidean domains, deep generative models, and game‑theoretic learning frameworks.
I hold an Honors B.Sc. in Mathematics and previously worked in EMEA Retail Strategy at Amazon Corporate. I am currently pursuing an M.S. in Data Science at George Washington University, where I conduct research under Dr. Junjun Yin, focusing on mathematically grounded approaches to learning in structured, high-dimensional systems.
I am seeking research opportunities at the intersection of neuromorphic architectures, spiking neural networks, non‑Euclidean representations, generative modeling, and non‑smooth dynamical games, with an emphasis on scalable, energy‑efficient learning systems.

Explaining Scenic Beauty Through Alexander's Properties of Living Structure
Working Paper [2026]
Sayan Patra, Junjun Yin
[Arxiv] [Website]

Abstract: This research bridges spatial design theory and computer vision to quantify the hidden geometric patterns that influence human perceptions of landscape beauty. By translating qualitative design principles into measurable image features, the framework aims to improve scenicness prediction models and condition generative AI architectures to produce more visually coherent landscapes. The findings will support future applications in urban planning, environmental design, and interpretable AI systems.

Neural Population Dynamics for Adaptive Robotic Control: A Bio-Inspired Hierarchical Learning Framework for Embodied Intelligence
Working Paper [2026]
Sayan Patra
[Paper] [Code]

Abstract: This research presents a brain-inspired robotic learning framework that integrates neural population dynamics and hierarchical control to improve robotic adaptability in dynamic environments. By decoding large-scale biological neural datasets into temporal dynamical models, the project translates sensorimotor principles into a robust, three-layer architecture for autonomous manipulation and navigation. Ultimately, this work establishes a computational bridge between neuroscience and real-world embodied intelligence.
