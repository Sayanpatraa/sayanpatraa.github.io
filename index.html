Explaining Scenic Beauty Through Alexander’s Properties of Living Structure: Initial Proposal
Working Paper
Sayan Patra, Junjun Yin
[Arxiv] [Website]

Abstract: Human perception of landscape beauty (“scenicness”) is known to correlate with obvious visual factors such as the presence of natural elements (e.g., mountains, water, vegetation) or general image quality (brightness, saturation, clarity). However, beyond these surface characteristics, the architect and design theorist Christopher Alexander hypothesized that certain deep structural patterns — which he termed the “properties of living structure” — imbue scenes with a sense of life and beauty. This proposal outlines a study to operationalize Alexander’s fifteen properties of living structure in a computational framework and test whether these properties can explain or predict human scenicness ratings of landscape photographs. The research will extract quantitative measures of each property from images and incorporate them into interpretable machine learning models of scenicness. We will compare the explanatory power of these theory-driven features with conventional image features (such as semantic content and deep neural network embeddings). We hypothesize that the inclusion of Alexander’s structural property mea- sures will improve our ability to predict scenicness and, more importantly, provide human-interpretable insights into why certain landscapes are found beautiful. The outcomes will bridge qualitative archi- tectural theory and quantitative data science, potentially guiding future design, planning, and AI effort towards what makes environments visually appealing.
